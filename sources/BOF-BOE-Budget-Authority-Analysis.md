The Central Question: Can the BOF Change the BOE Budget Number?
===============================================================

*[← Back to Index](Index.md)*

*An analysis of the competing legal opinions from the [Town Attorney](../sources/Legal%20Opinion%20BOF%20Authority%20to%20Act%20Upon%20the%20BOE%20Budget%20-%202026-02-17.pdf) and the [BOE's counsel](../sources/Shipman%20and%20Goodwin%20Budget%20memo%202.pdf), based on the [Andover Town Charter](https://charter2024.andoverct.info) and [Connecticut General Statutes](https://www.cga.ct.gov/current/pub/titles.htm).*

----------

This is a genuinely hard question. Having read the charter text carefully alongside both memos, here's what I think:

**The Shipman reading is the more natural plain-text reading of Section 802E standing alone.** The language creates an explicit exception — "may add to, delete from, or eliminate requests... *except that* the Board may *only* comment and make recommendations on the budget requests of the local Board of Education." The word "only" is doing real limiting work, and the "except that" construction deliberately separates the BOE from other departments. If the drafters intended equal authority over all budgets, they wouldn't have written an exception.

**But the O'Brien reading makes better structural sense when you read the Charter as a whole.** And this is where I think the balance ultimately tips. Here's why:

1. **[Section 802F](https://charter2024.andoverct.info/#Section802F) creates a problem for the Shipman reading.** It says the BOF "shall prepare a final budget that incorporates any recommended changes." If the BOF's recommendations regarding the BOE are truly non-binding, what does "incorporates" mean? Does the BOF put the BOE's original number in the final budget, or its recommended number? The Charter doesn't say. But the word "incorporates" most naturally means the recommended changes become part of the budget document itself — not that they exist as a sidebar commentary.

2. **[Section 803B](https://charter2024.andoverct.info/#Section803B) makes the BOF's number a one-way ceiling.** The Town Meeting "shall only have the power to reduce or modify, but not increase the budget or any portion thereof." So whatever number the BOF puts in the final budget for the BOE is the maximum the BOE can receive. If the BOF is forced to pass through the BOE's own number untouched, then no body in the entire process — not the BOF, not the Town Meeting, not the referendum voters — has the authority to *set* the BOE appropriation. The BOE would effectively appropriate its own funds, subject only to an up-or-down rejection at referendum. That's a remarkable degree of autonomy that the Charter doesn't seem to intend.

3. **[Section 803D](https://charter2024.andoverct.info/#Section803D) undermines the idea of a categorical prohibition.** After a budget failure, the BOF "shall review the rejected budget and present the same or a revised budget." No distinction is drawn between the BOE and Town portions. Both sides seem to agree the BOF can adjust the BOE number at this stage. But if the BOF fundamentally *lacks authority* over the BOE bottom line under 802E, where does it acquire that authority in 803D? The Charter doesn't grant any new power — it just says "review" and "revise." The more coherent reading is that the BOF always has this authority, and 803D is simply the procedural mechanism for re-exercising it.

4. **The "comment only" language has a plausible narrower meaning.** O'Brien suggests — and I find this credible — that 802E's exception is an imperfect attempt to codify the [C.G.S. 10-222](https://www.cga.ct.gov/current/pub/chap_170.htm#sec_10-222) principle that the BOF has no control over BOE *line items*. The BOF can comment on how the BOE allocates its money internally, but can't redirect spending from one BOE line to another. It *can* recommend (and set) a different bottom-line total. This reading gives the "comment and make recommendations" clause meaningful work to do without rendering 802F and 803B incoherent.

**Where Shipman's argument is weakest**, in my view, is the claim that the Town Meeting is "the authority making appropriations" under C.G.S. 10-222. That's technically true — the Town Meeting does vote to adopt the budget. But the Town Meeting can only *reduce*, never increase, the numbers the BOF gives it. A body that can only say "this much or less" is not really exercising independent appropriation authority in any meaningful sense. The BOF, which assembles and sets the ceiling of the entire budget, is functionally the fiscal authority even if the Town Meeting has formal final say.

**Where O'Brien's argument is weakest** is that it requires reading "only comment and make recommendations" as meaning something considerably more than commenting and recommending. That's a lot of weight to put on structural inference when the plain text seems deliberately restrictive.

**On balance**, I think the structural reading is more persuasive. A charter should be read as a coherent whole, and the Shipman reading creates a system where 802F's "incorporates any recommended changes" is largely meaningless for the biggest single item in the budget, and where no elected body other than the BOE itself can set the BOE appropriation level. That's an odd result for a town that chose to route its budget through a Board of Finance, a Town Meeting, *and* a referendum — three layers of democratic oversight that would all be toothless with respect to the largest line item.

But I want to emphasize: the Charter language genuinely is ambiguous, and reasonable attorneys can disagree. This is exactly the kind of question that, if it ever went to court, could go either way depending on how much weight the judge gives to plain text versus structural coherence.
